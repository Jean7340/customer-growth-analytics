Campaign Effectiveness Analysis: Measuring Incremental Revenue with a Geo Experiment

Author

Jean Liu

Business Context

A retailer launched a regional marketing campaign to evaluate whether advertising generated incremental revenue.

The campaign was tested through a geo-based experiment using Designated Market Areas (DMAs). Some markets were assigned to a treatment group, while others served as a control group.

This analysis estimates the causal impact of the campaign and evaluates whether the marketing investment should be scaled.

Business Question

Did the advertising campaign generate incremental revenue?

More specifically,

  • Was revenue growth driven by advertising?
  • Or were treatment markets already larger before the campaign?

Answering this question is critical for determining whether the campaign should be scaled, modified, or discontinued.

Experimental Design

The campaign was implemented as a geo experiment.

Selected Designated Market Areas (DMAs) received advertising, while comparable DMAs served as a control group.

Revenue was observed before and after the campaign.

The analysis estimates advertising incrementality by comparing revenue changes across treatment and control markets rather than relying on raw revenue differences.

Analysis workflow

Data Preparation

Show Code 
campaign <- read_csv("../data/Date of Advertising.csv")

campaign <- campaign |>
  mutate(
    date = as.Date(date, format = "%m/%d/%Y"),

    period = factor(
      isTreatmentPeriod,
      levels = c(0, 1),
      labels = c("Before", "After")
    ),

    group = factor(
      isTreatmentGroup,
      levels = c(0, 1),
      labels = c("Control", "Treatment")
    )
  )

summary_table <- campaign |>
  group_by(group, period) |>
  summarise(
    n_observations = n(),
    n_dmas = n_distinct(dma),
    avg_revenue = mean(revenue),
    median_revenue = median(revenue),
    total_revenue = sum(revenue),
    .groups = "drop"
  )

summary_table |>
  rename(
    Group = group,
    Period = period,
    Observations = n_observations,
    DMAs = n_dmas,
    `Average Revenue` = avg_revenue,
    `Median Revenue` = median_revenue,
    `Total Revenue` = total_revenue
  ) |>
  kable(
    digits = 0,
    caption = "Campaign Summary by Group and Period"
  )

Baseline Comparison

Before estimating campaign impact, we need to check whether treatment and control markets were comparable before the campaign.

Although treatment markets exhibited higher average revenue in raw values, the distribution of log-transformed revenue is broadly similar across groups. This observation is consistent with the formal balance check presented below.

Because raw revenue levels differ across markets, comparing post-campaign revenue alone could lead to misleading conclusions. A formal pre-campaign balance check and a Difference-in-Differences model are therefore used to isolate the incremental effect of advertising.

Pre-Campaign Balance Check

Before estimating campaign impact, we first check whether treatment and control markets were statistically similar during the pre-campaign period.

Naive Before–After Estimate

A common way to evaluate a marketing campaign is to compare revenue before and after the campaign within the treatment markets.

However, this approach assumes that all observed changes are caused by the campaign, ignoring other factors such as seasonality, macroeconomic conditions, and overall market trends.

The following regression estimates the average revenue difference before and after the campaign for treatment markets only.

Show Code 
treatment <- campaign |>
  filter(group == "Treatment")

before_after_model <- lm(
  log(revenue) ~ period,
  data = treatment
)

tidy(before_after_model) |>
  filter(term != "(Intercept)") |>
  transmute(
    Variable = "After Period",
    Estimate = round(estimate, 3),
    `P-value` = round(p.value, 3),
    Interpretation = "Revenue declined within treatment markets"
  ) |>
  kable(
    caption = "Naive Before–After Regression Results"
  )
Naive Before–After Regression Results
Variable Estimate P-value Interpretation
After Period -0.039 0.047 Revenue declined within treatment markets

Although revenue declined after the campaign within treatment markets (β = -0.039, p = 0.047), this estimate cannot be interpreted as the causal effect of advertising because it does not control for broader market trends.

The figure suggests that treatment and control markets followed similar revenue trends over time. To formally estimate whether the campaign generated incremental revenue beyond these common trends, we apply a Difference-in-Differences (DiD) model.

Show Code 
incrementality_model <- lm(
  log(revenue) ~ isTreatmentGroup * isTreatmentPeriod,
  data = campaign
)

results <- tidy(incrementality_model) |>
  filter(term != "(Intercept)") |>
  mutate(
    Variable = c(
      "Treatment",
      "Post Period",
      "Treatment × Post"
    ),
    Interpretation = c(
      "No significant baseline difference",
      "Overall revenue declined slightly",
      "No significant incremental advertising effect"
    )
  ) |>
  transmute(
    Variable,
    Estimate = round(estimate, 3),
    `P-value` = round(p.value, 3),
    Interpretation
  )

kable(
  results,
  caption = "Difference-in-Differences Regression Results"
)
Difference-in-Differences Regression Results
Variable Estimate P-value Interpretation
Treatment -0.014 0.571 No significant baseline difference
Post Period -0.046 0.096 Overall revenue declined slightly

The trend plot suggests that treatment and control markets experienced similar revenue changes over time. The Difference-in-Differences model formally estimates whether the treatment markets experienced additional revenue growth beyond this common trend. The interaction term captures this incremental advertising effect.

Business Recommendation

The Difference-in-Differences model found no statistically significant incremental advertising effect (β = 0.007, p = 0.845). Based on the current evidence, campaign expansion is not recommended.

Limitations

This analysis is based on a geo-level experiment, so the results may not generalize to all advertising campaigns.

Potential limitations include market-level differences, seasonality, regional demand shocks, and the fact that results from one campaign or channel may not apply to other media strategies.
